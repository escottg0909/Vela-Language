Part One

The Core Idea

Vela has no letters for sounds. Instead it has 18 root glyphs, each carrying one concept — light, fire, water, mind, motion — plus four special operator glyphs that transform meaning. Every word in the language is a chain of these roots.

Each root is spoken as exactly one syllable, so a word's spelling is its meaning and its pronunciation at the same time:

vave — star
va (light) + ve (sky)
"the light of the sky"
nakive — moon
na (opposite) + ki (fire) + ve (sky)
"the opposite of the sun"
Pronunciation stays trivial: every root uses only sounds English already has, and stress always lands on the second-to-last syllable — nakive → nah-KEE-veh, Vela → VEH-lah. One rule, zero exceptions.

Speaking in Sentences

A written sentence like Ka lu sa he is four separate words, not one long word — spoken Vela keeps that just as clear as it looks on the page:

Ka · lu · sa · he
kah — loo — sah — heh, each word given its own short beat, like counting

Three habits keep the words from running together:

1. Every word keeps its own stress. A multi-syllable word stresses its second-to-last syllable no matter what's next to it — nakive sa stays nah-KEE-veh sah, never blending into one four-syllable blob. Single-syllable words like lu or sa just get their own light beat.

2. Consonants at a word's edge don't merge into the next word. Say each word as its own clean unit, the way you'd say "big cat," not "bi-gcat." A tiny breath between words is enough — Vela has no contractions or elisions across word boundaries.

3. Sentence rhythm falls where the grammar falls. Particles (ka, zu, na, ne, vo) are unstressed and quick, almost like "a" or "the" in English; roots and built words carry the beat. That contrast — quick particle, solid root, quick particle, solid root — is what gives a Vela sentence its rhythm instead of a flat, unbroken string of syllables.

Read it like a list of short, separate words with natural pauses between them, and it will never sound like one long word — even a dense sentence like Zu la to lashi ne mishi vo? breaks cleanly into "zoo — lah — toh — LAH-shee — neh — MEE-shee — voh?"
Part Two

The 18 Root Concepts

Every glyph is a tiny picture of its idea, drawn in one or two fast pen strokes. Learn these 18 and you can read most of the language.

The 4 Operators

Operators don't mean anything alone — they transform the word they're inside. These are what make Vela generative: one operator can double your vocabulary.

Operator placement: na (opposite) always opens a word · shi (many) always closes a word · ma (great) and ni (small) sit wherever flows best. So na·ki = opposite-of-fire = cold, and vave·shi = star-many = stars.

Pronouns & Particles

These seven glyphs are not concepts to build words from — they're grammatical words in their own right (pronouns, tense, possession, questions), covered fully in Part Four. Ka, for instance, isn't a letter or a root; it's the complete word for "past," standing on its own before a verb. Shown together here purely for reference, the full glyph set is 18 roots + 4 operators + 7 particles = 29 in all.

Part Three

Building Words

Three composition rules govern every word in the language.

BUILD 1First root is the head, the rest describe it

Read a word left to right: the first glyph is what it is, everything after is what kind.

nu·lu water + motion → moving water → river
nu·ma water + great → sea
nu·so water + stillness → ice

Which order do I put the roots in? Ask yourself "which of these two ideas is the thing, and which one is just describing it?" The thing goes first, the description goes second — the head root sets the category, and everything after it narrows that category down.

Worked example — "see": the two ideas involved are sense (ze) and light (va). Ask which one is the head: "seeing" is fundamentally an act of sensing — light is just the thing being sensed, the description of what kind of sensing it is. So the head (sense) comes first: ze·va → zeva, "a sensing of light" → to see.

Flip the order — va·ze → vaze, "light + sense" — and you'd be building a different word, headed by light and modified by sense: something closer to "a light that perceives," which isn't a real Vela word. Same two roots, opposite order, different (or nonsense) meaning. Zela (sense + voice = to hear) follows the identical logic — sense is always the head when you're naming a sense-verb.
BUILD 2na flips a word to its opposite

The opposite operator inverts the meaning of everything after it. Half the dictionary is free:

va light → nava darkness · ki hot → naki cold · shavi friend → nashavi enemy · kive sun → nakive moon
BUILD 3Position in the sentence decides the word class

Roots aren't locked to being nouns or verbs. In the verb slot (first — see the grammar), a word acts; in a noun slot, it's a thing; after a noun, it describes.

nu as noun = water · as verb = to drink · Nu mi numa vo? — "Do I drink the sea?"
va as noun = light · as adjective = bright: vave va = a bright star

Part Four

The Grammar — Nine Rules, No Exceptions

Sentence grammar is unchanged from classical Vela: no conjugations, no genders, no articles, no irregular anything.

RULE 1The verb comes first

Sentences run Verb → Subject → Object.

Zeva mi vave. — literally "See I star" → "I see a star."
RULE 2Verbs never change — particles carry the tense

Put ka before the verb for past, zu for future. Nothing = present. Their glyphs are literally arrows pointing backward and forward in time.

Ka lu sa he. — "Past-go she home" → "She went home."
Zu lu sa he. — "She will go home."
RULE 3Negate with na

The same opposite-glyph, standing alone before the verb, negates the sentence. Inside a word it flips meaning; in front of a verb it means "not."

Na zu koha mishi ta. — "We will not eat dirt."
RULE 4Plural = close the word with shi
vave star → vaveshi stars · sha person → shashi people
RULE 5Adjectives follow their noun
vave va — "star bright" → a bright star
RULE 6There is no verb "to be"

Noun + description is a complete sentence on its own.

Vave va. — "The star is bright."
Mi sha. — "I am a person."
RULE 7Possession uses ne ("of")

The thing owned comes first: owned + ne + owner.

he ne mi — "home of me" → my home
lasha ne to — "name of you" → your name
RULE 8Questions end in vo
Me to lasha ne mi vo? — "Do you know my name?"
RULE 9No articles, no gender

There is no "a," "an," or "the," and no grammatical gender. Vave means "star," "a star," or "the star" — context does the work.

Part Six

Numbers

Numbers are the only pure-sound words in Vela — ancient roots with no deeper meaning. In writing, they're simple tally strokes: one mark per unit, and every bundle of five is struck through.

#Vela#Vela
0zon6lin
1kel7sura
2tan8oth
3mir9nev
4vok10tel
5hesh

Bigger numbers stack, largest first: tel-tan = 12, tan tel = 20, tan tel mir = 23. Numbers come before the noun they count: mir vave = three stars.
